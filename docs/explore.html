How do groups form? It depends which theory you ask.

Three interactive visualizations - one shared dataset - showing how CFT, GFT, and TST each produce a different answer to the same question.

Theory 1

CFT - The Threshold

In Consensus-Fracture Theory, a group forms only when every pairwise affinity in the group exceeds θ. One weak link breaks the group.

CFT • drag threshold to form groups

Drag the slider to see groups form and dissolve.
  • θ ≈ 0.85+ - all singletons; even tight pairs don't qualify
  • θ ≈ 0.72 - cluster A forms (its weakest link is A₁–A₃ = 0.72)
  • θ ≈ 0.65 - all three clusters fully formed
  • θ < 0.15 - cross-cluster links activate; large merged groups
Key insight: A group requires ALL pairwise affinities ≥ θ. The "weakest link" determines when the full cluster forms.

Theory 2

GFT - Agents in Motion

In Gradient Field Theory, agents don't jump into groups - they drift continuously toward affinity partners. Groups emerge from the final resting positions.

GFT • spring simulation

What's happening: Each pair has a target separation distance inversely proportional to their affinity. High-affinity pairs are "pulled together"; low-affinity pairs drift apart.
  • Unlike CFT, groups emerge from position, not a threshold
  • Overlapping membership is possible (two clusters may merge)
  • Higher k → tighter, faster clustering
  • The trajectory matters, not just the final state

Theory 3

TST - Hot and Cold

In Thermodynamic Social Theory, groups are Potts-model spin assignments. High temperature → random, fluid groups. Low temperature → stable, frozen clusters. Watch the phase transition.

Phase transition occurs near T ≈ 1.5. Below it: stable clusters form reliably. Above it: assignments fluctuate and groups blur.
  • T → 0: frozen - energy-minimizing group assignment
  • T → ∞: random - all spin assignments equally probable
  • Near the transition: power-law group size distributions
  • Same data as CFT/GFT - completely different mechanism
